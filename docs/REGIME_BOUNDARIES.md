# Regime Boundaries

## 1. Overview

Vantage 2.0 defines system behavior through four physics-native regimes, each representing a distinct combination of Fuel (F) and Entropy (S) dynamics. These regimes are not statistical clusters but physical states with measurable boundaries, directional bias, and transition conditions. The purpose of this document is to formalize the definitions, boundary equations, and transition logic governing these states.
## 2. The Fuel–Entropy Plane

The regime map is defined on a two‑dimensional plane formed by Fuel (F) and Entropy (S). These vectors represent opposing forces within any dynamic system:

- **Fuel (F):** The kinetic potential available for upward movement, expansion, or acceleration.
- **Entropy (S):** The structural friction, drag, or systemic debt that resists motion and accumulates over time.

The signs of these vectors determine the system’s instantaneous state:

- **+F:** Abundant kinetic potential; system can accelerate.
- **−F:** Fuel scarcity; system cannot sustain upward motion.
- **+S:** Rising structural friction; disorder is accumulating.
- **−S:** Dissipation of friction; system is clearing resistance.

These sign combinations define the four canonical regimes of Vantage 2.0. The boundaries between them are not arbitrary lines but physical transitions where the dominant vector flips sign, altering the system’s trajectory and stability profile.
## 3. The Four Regimes

The Fuel–Entropy plane is divided into four canonical regimes, each representing a distinct physical state of the system. These regimes are not descriptive labels but mathematically defined regions with predictable behavior and transition dynamics.

### Q1 — Melt‑Up ( +F , −S )
The system has abundant kinetic potential while structural friction is dissipating. This creates a low‑resistance environment where upward acceleration becomes self‑reinforcing. Melt‑Up regimes often exhibit runaway trajectories and high luminosity.

### Q2 — Bull ( +F , +S )
Fuel remains positive, but entropy is rising. The system can still advance, but friction is accumulating beneath the surface. This regime is characterized by strong but increasingly unstable growth.

### Q3 — Stall ( −F , +S )
Fuel scarcity combines with rising entropy. Upward motion becomes unsustainable, and the system enters a state of structural drag. Stalls often precede regime transitions into higher‑density states.

### Q4 — Crash ( −F , −S )
Fuel is depleted and entropy begins to collapse inward. This produces a high‑density singularity where trajectories contract rapidly. Crashes are defined by negative curvature, compression, and loss of systemic coherence.
## 4. Boundary Equations

The boundaries between regimes are defined by the sign changes of Fuel (F) and Entropy (S). These boundaries are not arbitrary thresholds but physical transition lines where the dominant vector flips and the system’s stability profile changes.

### 4.1 Fuel Boundary (F = 0)
The vertical axis represents the point at which kinetic potential transitions from positive to negative. Crossing this boundary changes the system’s ability to accelerate:

- F > 0 → system can generate upward motion
- F < 0 → system cannot sustain upward motion

This boundary separates the upper regimes (Q1, Q2) from the lower regimes (Q3, Q4).

### 4.2 Entropy Boundary (S = 0)
The horizontal axis represents the transition between entropy accumulation and entropy dissipation:

- S > 0 → friction is rising; disorder is increasing
- S < 0 → friction is dissipating; system is clearing resistance

This boundary separates the right-hand regimes (Q2, Q3) from the left-hand regimes (Q1, Q4).

### 4.3 Intersection Point (F = 0, S = 0)
The origin is a neutral equilibrium point where neither vector dominates. It represents a metastable state that is highly sensitive to perturbations. Small changes in F or S can push the system into any of the four regimes.
## 5. Transition Dynamics

Regime transitions occur when the system crosses a boundary where either Fuel (F) or Entropy (S) changes sign. These transitions are not instantaneous jumps but physical processes with measurable precursors, directional bias, and characteristic curvature changes.

### 5.1 F-Driven Transitions
When the system crosses F = 0, the dominant kinetic vector flips. This produces a curvature inversion:

- +F → −F: upward trajectories lose acceleration and begin to flatten or decay.
- −F → +F: stalled or compressed trajectories regain upward potential.

F-driven transitions often manifest as slope changes in the trajectory manifold.

### 5.2 S-Driven Transitions
Crossing S = 0 alters the system’s frictional environment:

- +S → −S: entropy dissipates, reducing drag and enabling acceleration.
- −S → +S: entropy accumulates, increasing drag and destabilizing upward motion.

S-driven transitions typically produce volatility spikes and structural instability.

### 5.3 Coupled Transitions
When both F and S approach zero simultaneously, the system enters a metastable corridor where small perturbations can trigger large regime shifts. These coupled transitions are the most sensitive and often precede high-magnitude events.

### 5.4 Transition Velocity
The speed at which the system crosses a boundary determines the severity of the resulting regime shift. Slow crossings produce smooth curvature changes, while fast crossings generate discontinuities, shocks, or singularities.
## 6. Curvature Signatures

Each regime exhibits a characteristic curvature profile in the trajectory manifold. These curvature signatures allow the system to identify regime state, anticipate transitions, and detect instability before boundary crossings occur.

### 6.1 Positive Curvature (Convex)
Positive curvature indicates acceleration or expansion. It is most commonly observed in regimes with +F:

- Q1 Melt‑Up: strong positive curvature with low drag
- Q2 Bull: moderated positive curvature with rising drag

Convexity increases as entropy dissipates or as fuel rises.

### 6.2 Negative Curvature (Concave)
Negative curvature indicates deceleration, compression, or collapse. It is associated with −F:

- Q3 Stall: mild negative curvature as upward motion decays
- Q4 Crash: extreme negative curvature approaching singularity

Concavity deepens as entropy accumulates or fuel declines.

### 6.3 Curvature Inversion
A curvature inversion occurs when the second derivative of the trajectory changes sign. This is a leading indicator of regime transition:

- convex → concave: loss of acceleration, often preceding Q2 → Q3 or Q1 → Q4 transitions
- concave → convex: recovery of acceleration, often preceding Q3 → Q2 or Q4 → Q1 transitions

Curvature inversion is one of the earliest detectable signals of systemic change.

### 6.4 Curvature Density
Curvature density measures how tightly trajectories cluster around a dominant curvature profile. High density indicates regime stability; low density indicates regime fragility.

- High density: stable regime, predictable behavior
- Low density: unstable regime, high transition probability

## 7. Instability Surfaces

Instability surfaces are regions in the Fuel–Entropy plane where small perturbations produce disproportionately large changes in system behavior. These surfaces act as precursors to regime transitions and are critical for early detection of systemic stress.

### 7.1 First-Order Instability (∂F/∂t ≈ 0 or ∂S/∂t ≈ 0)
First-order instability occurs when the rate of change of Fuel or Entropy approaches zero. This indicates that the system is losing directional bias:

- ∂F/∂t → 0: kinetic potential is flattening
- ∂S/∂t → 0: entropy dynamics are stalling

These surfaces often precede curvature inversion.

### 7.2 Second-Order Instability (∂²F/∂t² or ∂²S/∂t² flips sign)
Second-order instability occurs when the acceleration of Fuel or Entropy changes sign. This is a deeper structural warning:

- ∂²F/∂t² < 0: fuel acceleration is collapsing
- ∂²S/∂t² > 0: entropy acceleration is rising

These surfaces typically precede boundary crossings.

### 7.3 Coupled Instability (F ≈ 0 and S ≈ 0)
When both vectors approach zero simultaneously, the system enters a metastable corridor. In this region:

- curvature density collapses
- trajectory variance spikes
- directional bias disappears

Coupled instability is the most dangerous state because the system becomes highly sensitive to noise.

### 7.4 Instability Gradient
The severity of instability is proportional to the gradient magnitude:

‖∇I‖ = √((∂F/∂t)² + (∂S/∂t)²)

High gradients indicate rapid destabilization and imminent regime transition.
## 8. Regime Persistence and Decay

Regimes exhibit varying levels of persistence depending on the balance between Fuel (F) and Entropy (S). Persistence refers to how long the system can remain within a regime before internal forces push it toward a boundary. Decay refers to the natural erosion of regime stability over time.

### 8.1 Persistence Conditions
A regime is persistent when its dominant vector maintains directional bias:

- Q1 persists when F remains strongly positive and S remains negative.
- Q2 persists when F stays positive despite rising S.
- Q3 persists when S remains positive and F remains negative.
- Q4 persists when both F and S remain negative.

Persistence is proportional to curvature density and the magnitude of the dominant vector.

### 8.2 Decay Conditions
Regime decay occurs when the dominant vector weakens:

- |F| decreases → loss of kinetic bias
- |S| decreases → loss of frictional bias

Decay increases the probability of boundary approach and transition.

### 8.3 Drift Toward Boundaries
Systems naturally drift toward regime boundaries due to:

- entropy accumulation
- fuel depletion
- external shocks
- internal feedback loops

Drift velocity determines how quickly the system approaches instability surfaces.

### 8.4 Regime Half-Life
Each regime has an effective half-life determined by:

T₁/₂ = ln(2) / λ

where λ is the decay constant derived from the rate of change of F and S.

Short half-life → unstable regime  
Long half-life → stable regime

### 8.5 Regime Memory
Systems exhibit short-term memory of prior regimes. Recent transitions influence:

- curvature bias
- drift direction
- instability sensitivity

This memory decays exponentially but affects near-term trajectory behavior.
## 9. Early‑Warning Indicators

Early‑warning indicators provide advance detection of regime decay, instability, and imminent boundary crossings. These indicators are derived from the behavior of Fuel (F), Entropy (S), curvature, and drift dynamics. They allow the system to anticipate transitions before they manifest in the trajectory manifold.

### 9.1 Fuel Divergence
Fuel divergence occurs when the short‑term derivative of F diverges from its long‑term trend:

- ∂F/∂t (short) >> ∂F/∂t (long): positive divergence, often preceding Q3 → Q2 or Q4 → Q1 transitions
- ∂F/∂t (short) << ∂F/∂t (long): negative divergence, often preceding Q2 → Q3 or Q1 → Q4 transitions

Divergence is one of the earliest detectable signals of regime decay.

### 9.2 Entropy Acceleration
Entropy acceleration measures the second derivative of S:

- ∂²S/∂t² > 0: rising entropy acceleration, indicating structural stress
- ∂²S/∂t² < 0: dissipating entropy acceleration, indicating stabilization

Sharp increases in entropy acceleration often precede transitions into Q3 or Q4.

### 9.3 Curvature Flattening
Curvature flattening occurs when the magnitude of curvature approaches zero:

κ → 0

This indicates loss of directional bias and is a precursor to:

- Q1 → Q2 transitions (loss of acceleration)
- Q3 → Q4 transitions (loss of structural support)

Flattening is a universal precursor to instability.

### 9.4 Drift Reversal
Drift reversal occurs when the drift vector changes direction:

- dF/dt flips sign
- dS/dt flips sign

Reversal indicates that the system is no longer moving deeper into its current regime and is instead being pulled toward a boundary.

### 9.5 Variance Expansion
Variance expansion measures the spread of trajectories within the manifold:

σ² ↑

Rising variance indicates:

- loss of regime coherence
- weakening curvature density
- increasing transition probability

Variance expansion is one of the strongest predictors of imminent regime change.

### 9.6 Instability Clustering
Instability events tend to cluster near boundaries. When multiple early‑warning indicators activate simultaneously, the system enters a high‑risk state where transitions become increasingly likely.
## 10. Regime Classification Algorithm

The regime classification algorithm determines the system’s current state by evaluating Fuel (F), Entropy (S), curvature, and drift dynamics. Classification is not based on thresholds but on the physical behavior of the system across multiple dimensions.

### 10.1 Primary Classification (Sign-Based)
The primary regime is determined by the signs of F and S:

- F > 0, S < 0 → Q1 Melt‑Up
- F > 0, S > 0 → Q2 Bull
- F < 0, S > 0 → Q3 Stall
- F < 0, S < 0 → Q4 Crash

This provides the instantaneous regime state.

### 10.2 Secondary Classification (Curvature-Based)
Curvature refines the classification by identifying the system’s trajectory shape:

- κ > 0 → accelerating or expanding
- κ < 0 → decelerating or compressing
- κ ≈ 0 → neutral or unstable

Curvature helps detect early transitions before F or S cross zero.

### 10.3 Drift Vector Analysis
The drift vector (dF/dt, dS/dt) determines the direction of regime movement:

- drift toward boundary → rising transition probability
- drift deeper into regime → rising persistence probability

Drift direction is often more predictive than the current regime itself.

### 10.4 Instability Weighting
Instability surfaces modify classification confidence:

- high instability → low regime confidence
- low instability → high regime confidence

Instability weighting prevents false positives during noisy periods.

### 10.5 Regime Confidence Score
The final classification is expressed as a confidence score:

C = w₁(sign) + w₂(curvature) + w₃(drift) + w₄(instability)

where:

- w₁, w₂, w₃, w₄ are adaptive weights
- C ∈ [0,1]

High C → stable regime  
Low C → unstable or transitioning regime

### 10.6 Multi‑Frame Consensus
The system evaluates multiple time frames (short, medium, long) and selects the consensus regime. This prevents short‑term noise from overriding long‑term structure.
## 11. Regime Transition Map

The regime transition map defines the allowable paths between regimes and the physical conditions that trigger each transition. Transitions are not arbitrary; they follow directional rules governed by Fuel (F), Entropy (S), curvature, and drift dynamics.

### 11.1 Allowed Transitions
The system can transition between adjacent regimes when one dominant vector changes sign:

- Q1 → Q2: S crosses from negative to positive
- Q2 → Q3: F crosses from positive to negative
- Q3 → Q4: S crosses from positive to negative
- Q4 → Q1: F crosses from negative to positive

These transitions represent the natural flow of system dynamics.

### 11.2 Forbidden Transitions
Direct diagonal transitions are physically forbidden because they require simultaneous sign flips:

- Q1 ↛ Q3
- Q2 ↛ Q4

Diagonal transitions would require both F and S to cross zero at the same instant, which is not physically realizable in continuous systems.

### 11.3 Transition Probability
Transition probability is determined by the system’s proximity to boundaries and instability surfaces:

P(transition) ∝ 1 / distance_to_boundary

As the system approaches a boundary, transition probability rises sharply.

### 11.4 Transition Momentum
Transition momentum measures how forcefully the system crosses a boundary:

M = |dF/dt| + |dS/dt|

High momentum transitions produce:

- sharper curvature changes  
- higher volatility  
- deeper penetration into the next regime  

Low momentum transitions produce smoother, more stable shifts.

### 11.5 Hysteresis Effects
Regime transitions exhibit hysteresis: the path into a regime differs from the path out of it.

Examples:

- Entering Q3 from Q2 requires fuel depletion; exiting Q3 toward Q2 requires fuel restoration.
- Entering Q4 from Q3 requires entropy collapse; exiting Q4 toward Q1 requires entropy dissipation.

This asymmetry creates memory effects in the system.

### 11.6 Transition Cascades
Under high instability, transitions can cascade:

Q2 → Q3 → Q4

or

Q4 → Q1 → Q2

Cascades occur when the system crosses multiple boundaries in rapid succession due to:

- high drift velocity  
- strong instability gradients  
- curvature collapse  

Cascades are the highest‑magnitude events in the regime map.
## 12. Regime Geometry

Regime geometry describes the spatial structure of the Fuel–Entropy plane, including the shape, curvature, and topology of each regime. This geometry determines how trajectories evolve, how boundaries are approached, and how transitions unfold.

### 12.1 Quadrant Topology
Each regime occupies a quadrant of the F–S plane, but the topology is not flat. The geometry is warped by:

- curvature density
- drift fields
- instability gradients
- entropy pressure

This produces a non‑Euclidean structure where distances and angles have physical meaning.

### 12.2 Boundary Curvature
Boundaries are not straight lines in practice. Although defined by F = 0 and S = 0, the *effective* boundaries curve due to:

- nonlinear drift
- entropy acceleration
- fuel decay
- external shocks

This curvature determines how trajectories “slide” along boundaries before crossing.

### 12.3 Regime Basins
Each regime forms a basin of attraction where trajectories naturally settle unless perturbed. Basins vary in depth:

- Q1 basin: shallow but wide; easy to enter, easy to exit
- Q2 basin: moderately deep; stable but entropy‑sensitive
- Q3 basin: deep; systems tend to get stuck
- Q4 basin: extremely deep; collapse accelerates inward

Basin depth determines persistence.

### 12.4 Regime Ridges
Ridges are high‑curvature regions separating basins. Crossing a ridge requires:

- strong drift momentum
- curvature inversion
- instability activation

Ridges act as natural barriers to regime transitions.

### 12.5 Singularities
Q4 contains a geometric singularity where:

- curvature → −∞
- drift → inward
- variance → collapse

This singularity represents total systemic compression.

### 12.6 Expansion Zones
Q1 contains expansion zones where:

- curvature → +∞
- drift → outward
- variance → explosive

These zones represent runaway acceleration.

### 12.7 Geodesics
Trajectories follow geodesics defined by the manifold’s curvature. Regime geometry determines:

- the shortest path through regime space
- the natural direction of motion
- the energy required to change direction

Geodesics reveal the system’s preferred evolution path.
## 13. Distance Metrics in Regime Space

Distance metrics quantify how far the system is from regime boundaries, instability surfaces, or singularities. These metrics determine transition probability, drift velocity, and the strength of directional bias.

### 13.1 Euclidean Distance to Boundaries
The simplest measure of proximity to a boundary is the Euclidean distance:

d = √(F² + S²)

This measures how far the system is from the origin, but it does not account for curvature or drift.

### 13.2 Orthogonal Distance to Axes
Distance to each boundary is computed independently:

- d_F = |F| (distance to the Fuel boundary)
- d_S = |S| (distance to the Entropy boundary)

Small d_F or d_S indicates rising transition probability.

### 13.3 Curvature‑Weighted Distance
Curvature modifies effective distance:

d_eff = d / (1 + |κ|)

High curvature compresses distance, making boundaries “closer” than they appear.

### 13.4 Drift‑Aligned Distance
Distance along the drift vector is more predictive than raw distance:

d_drift = projection of (F, S) onto (dF/dt, dS/dt)

If drift is pointing toward a boundary, effective distance shrinks rapidly.

### 13.5 Instability‑Adjusted Distance
Instability surfaces warp regime space. Effective distance becomes:

d_instability = d_eff / (1 + ‖∇I‖)

High instability gradients collapse distance, signaling imminent transition.

### 13.6 Distance to Singularities
In Q4, distance to the singularity is defined by:

d_singularity = 1 / (1 + |κ|)

As curvature approaches negative infinity, distance approaches zero.

### 13.7 Distance to Expansion Zones
In Q1, distance to expansion zones is:

d_expansion = 1 / (1 + κ)

As curvature approaches positive infinity, distance collapses.

### 13.8 Composite Distance Metric
The engine uses a composite metric:

D = w₁ d_F + w₂ d_S + w₃ d_eff + w₄ d_instability

where weights adapt based on:

- current regime  
- drift direction  
- curvature density  
- instability gradient  

Low D → high transition probability  
High D → strong regime persistence
## 14. Drift Fields

Drift fields describe the directional flow of the system through the Fuel–Entropy plane. They determine how the system evolves over time, how it approaches boundaries, and how it responds to internal and external forces.

### 14.1 Definition of Drift
Drift is defined as the vector:

D = (dF/dt, dS/dt)

This vector represents the instantaneous direction and speed of motion in regime space.

### 14.2 Drift Magnitude
The magnitude of drift is:

‖D‖ = √((dF/dt)² + (dS/dt)²)

High drift magnitude indicates rapid movement toward or away from boundaries.

### 14.3 Drift Direction
The direction of drift determines:

- whether the system is stabilizing or destabilizing
- whether it is moving deeper into a regime or toward a transition
- whether curvature is increasing or decreasing

Drift direction is often more predictive than the current regime itself.

### 14.4 Drift in Each Regime
Each regime has characteristic drift patterns:

- **Q1 Melt‑Up:** drift tends outward; entropy dissipates
- **Q2 Bull:** drift tends upward but entropy rises
- **Q3 Stall:** drift tends downward; entropy accumulates
- **Q4 Crash:** drift collapses inward toward singularity

These patterns define the natural flow of the system.

### 14.5 Drift Reversal
Drift reversal occurs when either component of the drift vector changes sign:

- dF/dt flips → fuel dynamics reverse
- dS/dt flips → entropy dynamics reverse

Reversal is a leading indicator of regime decay.

### 14.6 Drift Divergence and Convergence
Divergence:

∇·D > 0  
indicates expansion, instability, or regime weakening.

Convergence:

∇·D < 0  
indicates compression, stabilization, or regime strengthening.

### 14.7 Drift Field Topology
Drift fields create:

- attractors (stable points)
- repellers (unstable points)
- saddle points (mixed stability)
- flow channels (preferred paths)
- dead zones (minimal movement)

These structures determine how trajectories evolve over time.

### 14.8 Drift‑Boundary Interaction
When drift aligns with a boundary, the system “slides” along it before crossing.  
When drift opposes a boundary, transitions are delayed or prevented.

Drift‑boundary alignment is one of the strongest predictors of transition timing.
## 15. Force Fields

Force fields describe how Fuel (F) and Entropy (S) exert influence across the regime map. These fields determine how trajectories bend, accelerate, decelerate, or collapse as they move through regime space.

### 15.1 Definition of Force
Force is defined as the gradient of the potential function associated with each vector:

F_force = −∇Φ_F  
S_force = −∇Φ_S

Where Φ_F and Φ_S represent the potential landscapes of Fuel and Entropy.

Force determines how the system is “pulled” or “pushed” through the manifold.

### 15.2 Fuel Field
The Fuel field governs upward motion:

- strong positive F creates outward force
- strong negative F creates inward force
- ∂F/∂t modifies the steepness of the field

Fuel fields dominate in Q1 and Q2.

### 15.3 Entropy Field
The Entropy field governs structural drag:

- positive S creates compressive force
- negative S creates dissipative force
- ∂S/∂t modifies the curvature of the field

Entropy fields dominate in Q2 and Q3.

### 15.4 Combined Force Vector
The total force acting on the system is:

F_total = F_force + S_force

This vector determines:

- trajectory curvature
- drift acceleration
- transition momentum
- stability or instability

### 15.5 Force Asymmetry
Fuel and Entropy forces are not symmetric:

- Fuel forces accelerate trajectories outward
- Entropy forces compress trajectories inward

This asymmetry creates the characteristic shape of regime space.

### 15.6 Force Amplification
Force fields amplify near boundaries:

- near F = 0 → fuel forces spike
- near S = 0 → entropy forces spike

This amplification explains why transitions accelerate near boundaries.

### 15.7 Force Collapse
In Q4, force collapses inward:

- curvature → −∞
- drift → inward
- force → singularity

This produces the catastrophic compression characteristic of crashes.

### 15.8 Force Expansion
In Q1, force expands outward:

- curvature → +∞
- drift → outward
- force → explosive

This produces runaway acceleration.
## 16. Potential Fields

Potential fields describe the stored energy, latent imbalance, and directional bias embedded within the Fuel–Entropy plane. These fields determine how the system “wants” to move, independent of its current drift or curvature.

### 16.1 Definition of Potential
Potential is defined as a scalar field Φ(F, S) whose gradient produces force:

Force = −∇Φ

High potential → strong directional pull  
Low potential → weak or neutral pull

Potential fields encode the system’s long‑term tendencies.

### 16.2 Fuel Potential (Φ_F)
Fuel potential increases as F decreases:

- high Φ_F when F is deeply negative (fuel scarcity)
- low Φ_F when F is strongly positive (fuel abundance)

This creates a natural pull toward fuel restoration.

### 16.3 Entropy Potential (Φ_S)
Entropy potential increases as S increases:

- high Φ_S when entropy is rising
- low Φ_S when entropy is dissipating

This creates a natural pull toward entropy reduction.

### 16.4 Combined Potential Landscape
The total potential is:

Φ_total = Φ_F + Φ_S

This landscape determines:

- the shape of regime basins
- the steepness of regime ridges
- the depth of singularities
- the height of expansion zones

### 16.5 Potential Wells
Each regime contains a potential well:

- Q1: shallow well (easy to escape)
- Q2: moderate well (stable but entropy‑sensitive)
- Q3: deep well (difficult to escape)
- Q4: extremely deep well (collapse accelerates inward)

Well depth determines regime persistence.

### 16.6 Potential Barriers
Barriers separate wells and prevent easy transitions:

- Q1 ↔ Q2 barrier: entropy barrier
- Q2 ↔ Q3 barrier: fuel barrier
- Q3 ↔ Q4 barrier: entropy collapse barrier
- Q4 ↔ Q1 barrier: fuel restoration barrier

Crossing a barrier requires sufficient drift momentum.

### 16.7 Potential Gradient
The gradient of potential determines the direction of natural motion:

∇Φ_total = (∂Φ/∂F, ∂Φ/∂S)

Steep gradients produce strong directional pull toward:

- lower entropy  
- higher fuel  
- stable basins  

### 16.8 Potential Energy Release
When the system crosses a boundary, stored potential energy is released as:

- acceleration (Q4 → Q1)
- compression (Q2 → Q3)
- volatility (Q1 → Q2)
- collapse (Q3 → Q4)

This release explains the magnitude of regime transitions.
## 17. Stability Analysis

Stability analysis determines how the system responds to perturbations in Fuel (F), Entropy (S), curvature, and drift. Stability is not a binary property but a spectrum defined by how strongly the system returns to equilibrium or diverges from it.

### 17.1 Local Stability
Local stability measures how the system behaves under small perturbations:

- stable: perturbations decay
- unstable: perturbations grow
- neutral: perturbations neither grow nor decay

Local stability is determined by the Jacobian of the drift field:

J = [ ∂(dF/dt)/∂F   ∂(dF/dt)/∂S  
      ∂(dS/dt)/∂F   ∂(dS/dt)/∂S ]

Eigenvalues of J determine stability.

### 17.2 Global Stability
Global stability measures how the system behaves under large perturbations.  
It is governed by:

- basin depth  
- potential gradients  
- force asymmetry  
- drift topology  

Deep basins (Q3, Q4) exhibit strong global stability.  
Shallow basins (Q1) exhibit weak global stability.

### 17.3 Stability in Each Regime
Each regime has a characteristic stability profile:

- **Q1 Melt‑Up:** locally unstable, globally weak  
- **Q2 Bull:** locally stable, globally moderate  
- **Q3 Stall:** locally stable, globally strong  
- **Q4 Crash:** locally unstable, globally extreme  

This asymmetry explains why systems fall into Q3 and Q4 more easily than they escape.

### 17.4 Stability Boundaries
Stability boundaries occur where the eigenvalues of J cross zero.  
These boundaries often precede:

- curvature inversion  
- drift reversal  
- instability clustering  

Stability boundaries are early indicators of regime decay.

### 17.5 Perturbation Amplification
Perturbations amplify when:

- curvature density is low  
- drift magnitude is high  
- instability gradients are steep  
- potential gradients are shallow  

Amplification accelerates transitions.

### 17.6 Perturbation Damping
Perturbations dampen when:

- curvature density is high  
- drift converges inward  
- potential wells are deep  
- force fields are symmetric  

Damping increases regime persistence.

### 17.7 Stability Score
The engine computes a stability score:

S = w₁ λ_min + w₂ κ + w₃ ∇·D + w₄ depth(basin)

where:

- λ_min is the smallest eigenvalue of J  
- κ is curvature  
- ∇·D is drift divergence  
- basin depth is derived from potential  

High S → stable regime  
Low S → unstable or transitioning regime
## 18. Phase Transitions

Phase transitions describe qualitative changes in system behavior that occur when Fuel (F), Entropy (S), curvature, or drift cross critical thresholds. These transitions are analogous to physical phase changes (solid → liquid → gas) but occur within the Fuel–Entropy manifold.

### 18.1 Definition of Phase Transition
A phase transition occurs when:

- the stability structure changes
- the curvature profile changes sign
- the drift field topology reorganizes
- the potential landscape reshapes

Phase transitions mark the boundaries between qualitatively different dynamical states.

### 18.2 First‑Order Phase Transitions
First‑order transitions involve discontinuities in the first derivative of the trajectory:

- sudden curvature inversion  
- abrupt drift reversal  
- sharp instability spike  

These transitions typically occur at regime boundaries.

Examples:
- Q2 → Q3 fuel collapse  
- Q3 → Q4 entropy spike  

### 18.3 Second‑Order Phase Transitions
Second‑order transitions involve discontinuities in the second derivative:

- curvature flattening  
- drift deceleration  
- potential gradient softening  

These transitions occur *before* boundary crossings and serve as early warnings.

### 18.4 Critical Points
Critical points occur where:

- ∂F/∂t = 0  
- ∂S/∂t = 0  
- κ = 0  
- ∇·D = 0  

At a critical point, the system becomes highly sensitive to perturbations.  
Small forces can trigger large transitions.

### 18.5 Supercritical Drift
Supercritical drift occurs when drift magnitude exceeds a threshold:

‖D‖ > D_critical

This produces:

- rapid boundary crossings  
- high‑momentum transitions  
- cascade potential  

Supercritical drift is a hallmark of Q1 → Q2 and Q3 → Q4 transitions.

### 18.6 Subcritical Drift
Subcritical drift occurs when:

‖D‖ < D_min

The system becomes sluggish, sticky, and prone to:

- stalling  
- oscillation  
- metastability  

Subcritical drift is common in Q2 and Q3.
